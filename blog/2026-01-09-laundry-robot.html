We don't want your laundry-folding robot that can't fold laundry. Hope this helps.

CES used to stand for “Consumer Electronics Show”, but after this year's CES, maybe we should think of a new name for the AI slopfest and niche-hobby-for-billionaires circlejerk.

I did not attend CES—I'd recommending listening to Ed Zitron's podcast for more information on these topics. I just couldn't bring myself to wade through the sea of sycophantic articles from tech journals with headlines that straight-up lie about what was on display, so there won't be many links or references here.

But, being a normal person with a love of consumer electronics, it really hit home for me after hearing all of the reports coming out of CES 2026 that the big problem with the consumer tech industry (well, one of the big problems) is this:

We (non-billionaire consumers) don't… really want products, anymore?

Hear me out.

TVs today are huge and paper-thin. They have mind-blowing color displays, resolution, and frame rates. We did it, folks. We TV-ed as hard as you can TV. You can only make a picture look so good before your job, as the tech company creating the medium by which to distribute the creative work of others, is over.

[George Bush giving a thumbs-up on an aircraft carrier under the banner "Mission Accomplished"]

But we need to sell new TVs, so we need to convince people that there is still something wrong with their current TVs, that this industry is “innovating” and worthwhile.

So let's slap a chatbot on a television and call it an innovation.

Our phones are peak. They're powerful little computers in our pockets that can connect us to others instantly no matter where in the world we are. There is nothing left for Apple or Android to sell us—we've already got what we want. Probably the only thing that would get me to buy a new phone (beyond planned obsolescence making it necessary) is better battery life. But that's not as exciting as when the first iPhone came out. I think these companies want to convince us that right now is as exciting a time in consumer electronics, and it simply isn't.

So let's slap a chatbot on a phone and call it an innovation. And let's convince everyone that they need this new product, that if they don't have a chatbot on their phone then do they really have a phone at all? Let's also pretend that consumers want to be constantly flooded with advertisements tailored to their data footprint, that we all haven't noticed that the Internet sucks now, Google sucks now, YouTube sucks now, fucking everything sucks now because it's all one big ad. Keep pretending that we're stupid. That'll go well.

Sticking with consumer electronics, I really think our homes are as automated as we want them to be. No one is excited about a laundry-folding robot. Why would I want to “save time” folding laundry in my own home if it means I need to break my back at work to afford the robot that does the easier, more fulfilling thing?

Can't you send my robot to work, while I putz around the house doing laundry and cooking and reading and making art and sitting outside staring at the sky having thoughts both profound and charmingly mundane, you know, just generally doing things that make people feel good if they have the time and energy to do them?

Laundry can be meditative. It connects you to yourself, your home, your family. These are the clothes you wear every day. They touch nearly every inch of surface area on the largest organ in your body. They are some of the most intimate items you can imagine. Everyone likes the smell of clean laundry, but you know what's better? The smell of a shirt worn recently or often by someone you love.

The bad part about laundry isn't doing the laundry. It's having to do the laundry after working 40 hours a week (if you're lucky), after paying the bills, after getting off the phone with the health insurance company denying your prescriptions, after being abused by a company that can fire anyone at any time for no reason with impunity, after watching your 401k disappear because some fuckhead is playing chicken with the stock market, after scrolling through social media and seeing a mother murdered by a death squad in this country you've always been told is the best in the world.

After all of that, laundry can feel like just one thing too fucking far.

But products can't solve those other problems. No tech company can make a product that stops children from being gunned down in the classroom. (Though we've tried—check out the charming trend of bulletproof backpacks.) No product will ensure you and your family have access to doctors and medications—but we can make a chatbot that will spit out randomly-generated garbage that looks sort of like medical advice! No product will strengthen labor laws to protect you from being fired at a whim. No product will make you feel like things are worthwhile and okay when they're not. (I mean, maybe heroin, temporarily. Have we tried that yet?)

But hey. Maybe we can make a robot that at the very least folds your child's laundry. So then, instead of you pulling the Paw Patrol-brand pajama shirt out of the dryer, holding it in your hands, remembering when you bought it or someone gave it to you at your future kid's baby shower, feeling the fabric that gets rougher with every wash but your kid still loves the thing, damn it, and what an amazing kid they are that they can love something so simple no matter its flaws—

Instead of that, we can burn money and carbon trying to build a machine that will think and feel none of those things, taking your place in your home while you spend more time on the screens that are giving you psychosis and all the while you continue to work, and work, and fucking work. You're more productive than ever, but your workload and hours and salary never seem to reflect that—and this is apparently a future we're supposed to be excited about.

I can hear some of you now. “But what about people with disabilities!”

First of all, I think it's pretty disgusting that people are suddenly using this as a rallying cry to justify use of technology that has shown absolutely no evidence of assisting disabled people. All of you who pull this half-baked argument out of your back pocket whenever someone insults your favorite widdle toy should be ashamed. You're just throwing darts in the dark trying to strike something that might one day be sort-of true—but is just as likely to be the exact opposite of helpful and assistive.

Did anyone ask a disabled person if something like a laundry-folding robot would help them? Do they want a stupid, massive, heavy, expensive, fragile robot that will take years to come to production, if ever, that right now can only do things like take a towel out of the dryer and try to fold it for seven minutes and only get partway there? What is the use case? What parts of the laundry process are difficult for people with different disabilities (they're not a monolith), and what is the most efficient and useful way to address them while preserving human dignity and foregrounding the aspects of the process that are fulfilling, worthwhile, good for us?

(Not that I'm trying to like, sanctify laundry. You can hate laundry. If you do, you probably also hate the laundry-folding robot, which as of right now does not fold laundry.)

If I had to pull something out of my ass, I'd think one option would look more like a laundry machine or dryer with some kind of extended chute for people with limited mobility, so they don't have to be as tall or reach as far or have as much dexterity or grip strength to perform the task.

What is the actual barrier? It's not just “laundry”. I don't know if it's cool to look at a group of people and say “You can't do laundry anymore at all. You're welcome. Nobel Prize, please.”

[GIF of Stephen Colbert on the Colbert Show saying "Nobel Prize please"]

Ugh. I am not qualified to speak any further on that, so we'll move on.

This is especially infuriating because there are legitimate avenues of research and experimentation in the tech world that would actually help people. I have Type 1 diabetes—maybe one of you guys with all the money could improve my CGM so it stops giving me false lows and false highs, stops crapping out altogether, isn't a huge plastic disc I have to shoot into my arm every 10 days that gives me a rash from the adhesive and ruins pictures where I wear short sleeves?

But while that would technically be a product, it's not one that can be marketed to everyone, from toddlers to grandparents, it's not something that you can convince the general public they simply cannot do without, and so medical tech limps along (in the United States at least, take a look at China and Japan if you think medical innovations have stalled worldwide) while companies that are somehow “worth” hundreds of billions of dollars while also being hundreds of billions in debt fire their engineers and developers and coders, try to sell us a shit product made of shit that does shit-all, and make us feel crazy for not wanting it!

Now that I write this, I'm shocked they haven't tried to shove an LLM into my continuous glucose monitor. Oh god, that's next, isn't it?

A quote attributed to the Marxist philosophers Frederic Jameson and Slavoj Žižek states: “It's easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism.”

I think that's where we are, now more than ever. Capitalism isn't doing anything for us, and we refuse to acknowledge it. Infinite growth is a lie. There comes a certain point when products and services can't really get any better—but you can't have that plateau line in your company books, even though to any sane person it would signal success.

We simply cannot fucking conceive of a world where we aren't plagued by advertisements and data miners, where we don't have to worry about making something profitable but instead just making something good. Even when it's staring us in the face! Even when average consumers are confused about what the hell we're supposed to be doing with these shitty apps!

We started off killing the planet to make things we needed. Now we're killing the planet just for the fucking hell of it, I guess, for something hardly any of us want or have any use for that doesn't actively make the world a worse place.

I don't know. Maybe we don't need a CES next year—it feels like they've taken the “consumer” out of “consumer electronics”. None of this crap is for us. It never will be again.

I've gotta sign off—I've got laundry to fold. Man, I sure wish I had a shitty robot to do it shittily instead. And maybe a shitty robot to write my Substack posts, again shittily, so I could then presumably go do… Whatever the fuck is left for humans to do.
